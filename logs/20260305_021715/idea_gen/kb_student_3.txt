### **Overview**
The paper addresses the challenge of maintaining reliable wireless connections and accurate target tracking in **high-mobility 6G networks** (e.g., Vehicle-to-Everything or V2X communication). The primary difficulty in these scenarios is the rapid variation of wireless channels and the frequent blockage of the Line-of-Sight (LoS) path.

The authors propose a novel framework that utilizes a **Channel Knowledge Map (CKM)** to bridge two distinct tracking domains: the **Coordinate Domain (C-Domain)** (location/velocity of the vehicle) and the **Beam Domain (B-Domain)** (Angles of Arrival/Departure). By coupling these domains, the system can track targets even when the LoS path is blocked by exploiting Non-Line-of-Sight (NLoS) multipath reflections.

---

### **Core Methodology**

The framework operates on a dual-domain loop where information is exchanged reciprocally via the CKM.

#### **1. CKM-Assisted Coordinate Domain (C-Domain) Tracking**
*   **Objective:** Track the vehicle's position and velocity using an Extended Kalman Filter (EKF).
*   **LoS Scenario:** When LoS is present, the system uses standard geometric measurements (delay, Doppler, and AoA of the LoS path) to update the state.
*   **NLoS Scenario (Innovation):** When LoS is blocked, traditional methods fail. Here, the system uses the **CKM** as a measurement model. The CKM maps specific geographic locations to expected multipath channel parameters. The system compares the *observed* NLoS path parameters (delay, Doppler, angle) against the CKM to infer the vehicle's location, effectively turning environmental scatterers into virtual anchors.

#### **2. CKM-Assisted Beam Domain (B-Domain) Tracking**
*   **Objective:** Track and predict the Angles of Arrival (AoA) for multiple signal paths to maintain beam alignment.
*   **Modeling:** Uses a discrete Markov process to model angle transitions.
*   **Adaptive Transition Probability Matrix (TPM):** The prediction of where the beam will move next is based on two sources fused together:
    1.  **Temporal Correlation:** How the angle changed in the previous time slot.
    2.  **CKM Prior:** The predicted *location* from the C-Domain is fed into the CKM to retrieve *a priori* knowledge of expected angles. This allows the system to predict sudden, non-linear jumps in beam angles caused by the environment (e.g., a path suddenly reflecting off a building).

#### **3. Predictive Beamforming (BF) Design**
*   **Objective:** Design the transmitted signal for the *next* time slot to maximize sensing accuracy.
*   **Optimization:** The authors formulate an optimization problem to minimize the maximum **Cramér-Rao Bound (CRB)** of the AoA estimation across multiple paths.
*   **Two-Step Approach:**
    1.  **Beam Selection:** Choose beams based on the hard predictions of AoAs from the B-Domain.
    2.  **Power Allocation:** Solve a convex optimization problem to distribute power among selected beams, ensuring even weak NLoS paths are tracked accurately.

### **Key Takeaways**

1.  **Robustness in NLoS Environments:** The most significant contribution is the ability to maintain continuous tracking when the LoS path is blocked. By using the CKM to interpret NLoS reflections, the system prevents tracking failure during blockages, which is a common failure point in conventional radar/communications schemes.
2.  **Reciprocal Aid Between Domains:** The C-Domain and B-Domain mutually enhance each other. High-quality beam tracking improves location estimation, while accurate location prediction (via CKM) provides "prior" knowledge that stabilizes beam tracking against noise or bursty channel changes.
3.  **Superior Tracking Accuracy:** Simulation results demonstrate that the proposed scheme significantly outperforms state-of-the-art baselines. It avoids beam misalignment and maintains lower Root Mean Square Error (RMSE) for position tracking in complex urban environments.
4.  **Optimized Sensing-Communication Trade-off:** The predictive beamforming design effectively compensates for the high propagation loss of NLoS paths. By optimizing power allocation based on predicted estimation errors, the system ensures that "weaker" paths are not lost, preserving the multipath information required for CKM-assisted positioning.

### **Overview**
The paper addresses the challenge of constructing a complete **Channel Knowledge Map (CKM)**—a database of signal quality at specific locations—using only sparse, incomplete measurements. While traditional methods rely on interpolation or auxiliary environmental data (like building maps), this paper proposes using **Generative AI** to treat the reconstruction as an image inpainting problem, filling in missing signal data based on learned global patterns.

### **Core Methodology**

**1. Problem Formulation as an Inverse Problem**
The authors frame CKM construction as a linear inverse problem ($\mathbf{y} = \mathbf{H}\mathbf{x}$), where they attempt to recover a complete channel map ($\mathbf{x}$) from a partially observed map ($\mathbf{y}$) and a mask ($\mathbf{H}$). Because the mask results in missing data, the problem is ill-posed (rank-deficient).

**2. Conditional Decoupled Diffusion Model (DDM)**
Instead of standard interpolation, the authors employ a specific type of generative model called a **Decoupled Diffusion Model (DDM)**.
*   **Decoupling Strategy:** Unlike standard diffusion (DDPM) which mixes noise and image data directly, DDM separates the process into two mappings: *image-to-zero* (attenuation) and *zero-to-noise*. This allows for faster sampling and better control.
*   **Architecture:**
    *   **VAE (Variational Autoencoder):** Maps the image pixel space to a latent space to reduce computational load and training time.
    *   **Swin-B Encoder:** Extracts multi-level features from the partially observed (masked) input image to condition the generation.
    *   **UNet:** Acts as the denoising network, predicting the missing information based on the conditional features.

**3. Training & Dataset**
*   The model learns the prior distribution of channel data ($q(\mathbf{x})$) rather than relying on mathematical assumptions.
*   It was trained and tested on the **CKMImageNet** dataset (ray-tracing data generated via Wireless Insite), which accurately simulates electromagnetic wave interactions (reflections, diffractions) in urban environments.

### **Key Takeaways**

**1. Superiority Over Interpolation**
The generative approach drastically outperforms traditional interpolation methods (KNN, Kriging, Bilinear, RBF).
*   *Quantitative Example:* In scenarios where buildings were masked, the proposed method achieved an **MSE (Mean Squared Error) of ~427**, whereas the best interpolation method (Kriging) had an MSE of ~1868.
*   *Qualitative:* The diffusion model successfully reconstructed complex "shadowing" effects caused by buildings, which interpolation methods generally smoothed over or missed entirely.

**2. Independence from Environmental Data**
A significant advantage of this method is that it **does not require auxiliary data** such as building layouts or transmitter locations. It reconstructs the map solely based on the patterns found in the sparse signal observations.

**3. Base Station (BS) Localization**
The method demonstrated a strong capability to "hallucinate" accurate data in large missing regions. Even when the area containing the Base Station was completely masked, the model could predict the high-signal "hotspot" of the BS with a positioning error of only **3.30 meters**, compared to 8–13 meters for other methods.

**4. Handling Complex Scenarios**
The model proved robust in two distinct difficult scenarios:
*   **Masking Buildings:** Predicting signal values in areas blocked by obstacles (non-line-of-sight).
*   **Not Masking Buildings:** Predicting values in open space based on sparse sampling.

In summary, the authors successfully demonstrate that **conditional diffusion models** can solve the ill-posed problem of CKM construction more accurately than statistical interpolation by learning the underlying physical laws of wave propagation from data.

### **Context & Problem Statement**
The paper addresses the challenge of constructing **Channel Knowledge Maps (CKMs)**—site-specific databases that provide wireless channel information (like signal strength, Angle of Arrival, etc.) for specific locations. CKMs are crucial for "environment-aware" 6G wireless networks.

However, obtaining complete CKMs is difficult because:
1.  **High Cost:** Running ray-tracing simulations or conducting physical drive tests for every location is expensive.
2.  **Incomplete Data:** Real-world measurements are sparse, discrete, and noisy.
3.  **Missing Environmental Data:** Existing AI methods often require detailed physical maps (building layouts) to generate radio maps, which may not always be available.

 The authors formulate CKM construction as a linear **ill-posed inverse problem** ($y = Ax + n$), where the goal is to recover the full map $x$ from degraded observations $y$.

---

### **Core Methodology: CKMDiff**
The authors propose **CKMDiff**, a generative AI framework based on conditional diffusion models.

**1. Mathematical Framework (Inverse Problems via Diffusion):**
Instead of using simple interpolation (like Kriging) or deterministic regression (like standard CNNs), CKMDiff treats the construction task as generating data from a learned probability distribution. It uses the **Decoupled Diffusion Model (DDM)** approach, which separates the image degradation process into signal attenuation and noise addition. This allows the model to iteratively reverse the degradation to recover the clean map.

**2. Architecture (Conditional Latent Diffusion):**
*   **Latent Space:** To save computational power, the model uses a Variational Autoencoder (VAE) to compress channel maps into a lower-dimensional *latent space* (similar to Stable Diffusion) before processing.
*   **Conditioning:** The model is **conditional**. It takes the limited/noisy on-site measurements as input to guide the generation process, ensuring the output matches the known reality while hallucinating the missing parts based on learned physics.
*   **Network Structure:** It uses a modified **UNet** architecture. A **Swin Transformer** encoder extracts features from the observed sparse data, which are then injected into the UNet decoder to control the diffusion process.

**3. Environment-Aware Feature Extraction (No Physical Maps Required):**
A key innovation is that CKMDiff **does not require a CAD map** of the city. Instead, it infers geometry from the sparse radio data itself:
*   It performs image binarization on the incomplete channel data (identifying areas with no signal as likely obstacles).
*   It applies **Canny edge detection** to extract structural boundaries from the sparse data.
*   These extracted features are combined into an RGB-like image and fed into the model, allowing it to learn implicit relationships between building layouts and signal propagation.

---

### **Key Takeaways & Results**

**1. Unified Solution for Multiple Tasks:**
The unified mathematical model ($y = Ax + n$) allows CKMDiff to perform three distinct tasks using the same framework:
*   **Denoising:** Cleaning up noisy measurements.
*   **Inpainting:** Filling in large missing regions (gaps in data).
*   **Super-resolution:** Upscaling low-resolution data to high-resolution maps.

**2. Superior Performance:**
Evaluated on two datasets (**CKMImageNet** and **RadioMapSeer**), CKMDiff significantly outperformed benchmarks, including:
*   **Traditional methods:** KNN, Kriging, and Bilinear Interpolation.
*   **Deep Learning methods:** cGAN (Conditional Generative Adversarial Networks).
*   **Metrics:** It achieved the lowest Mean Squared Error (MSE) and Fréchet Inception Distance (FID), and the highest PSNR and SSIM scores.

**3. Robustness to Complexity:**
The model proved effective even on the complex **CKMImageNet** dataset, which is based on high-fidelity ray tracing (including reflections and diffractions) rather than simple dominant-path models. This suggests the diffusion model successfully "learned" the physics of electromagnetic wave propagation.

**4. Practicality:**
By eliminating the dependency on auxiliary physical environment maps (which are hard to get) and transceiver locations, CKMDiff is more practical for real-world deployment than previous radio mapping AI techniques.
