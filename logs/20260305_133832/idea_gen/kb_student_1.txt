### **Core Methodology**
The paper provides a comprehensive comparative study of **iterative linear detectors** designed to bypass the high computational cost of exact matrix inversion (which has a complexity of $O(K^3)$) in M-MIMO uplink detection. 

The authors evaluate seven primary iterative methods:
1.  **Neumann Series (NS):** Approximates matrix inversion using polynomial expansion.
2.  **Gauss-Seidel (GS):** Solves linear equations iteratively by decomposing the Gram matrix into triangular components.
3.  **Successive Over-Relaxation (SOR):** An accelerated version of GS using a relaxation parameter ($\omega$).
4.  **Jacobi (JA):** A parallelizable iterative method, though often slower to converge than GS.
5.  **Conjugate Gradient (CG):** Refines the solution by moving along conjugate directions to minimize error.
6.  **Richardson (RI):** Exploits the symmetry of the Gram matrix and utilizes a relaxation parameter.
7.  **Optimized Coordinate Descent (OCD):** Performs coordinate-wise updates to achieve a near-optimal solution for convex optimization.

**Evaluation Framework:**
*   **Performance:** Measured via Bit-Error-Rate (BER) across different Signal-to-Noise Ratios (SNR).
*   **Complexity:** Analyzed through the number of real multiplications and additions.
*   **Hardware Efficiency (HE):** Evaluated by implementing the designs on a Xilinx Virtex-7 FPGA and calculating the ratio of throughput to Normalized Resource Consumption (NRC).
*   **Scenarios:** Tested under varying ratios of Base Station (BS) antennas to User Terminals (UTs) ($\beta$), different iteration counts ($n$), and different relaxation parameters ($\omega$).

### **Core Methodology**

The paper addresses the challenges of Extra-Large MIMO (XL-MIMO) systems—specifically computational complexity, scalability, and spatial non-stationarity—by proposing a **fully decentralized and uncoordinated receiver architecture.**

1.  **Decentralized Architecture (LPU-based):** Instead of a central processing unit (CPU) managing the entire array, the system is divided into $B$ sub-arrays. Each sub-array is controlled by a **Local Processing Unit (LPU)**. These LPUs operate in parallel and only exchange information with their immediate neighbors, eliminating the need for a high-bandwidth central controller.
2.  **Hybrid Message-Passing Framework:** The receiver uses a combination of two approximate inference techniques:
    *   **Mean-Field (MF) / Variational Message Passing (VMP):** Used locally within each LPU to estimate user symbols and noise precision based on the sub-array's observed signal.
    *   **Belief Propagation (BP):** Used to exchange "messages" (beliefs) between adjacent LPUs. This ensures that different sub-arrays eventually converge on a consistent estimate of the transmitted symbols.
3.  **Local Successive Interference Cancellation (SIC):** The algorithm incorporates a "local SIC" mechanism. Each LPU calculates a Likelihood Ratio (LR) for each user. If a user’s signal is strong enough (exceeding a threshold $\Gamma_{thr}$), the LPU makes a "hard decision" on that symbol and cancels its interference locally. This decision is then propagated to neighboring sub-arrays to improve their detection performance.
4.  **Spatial Non-Stationarity (NS) Awareness:** The methodology is specifically designed to exploit **Visibility Regions (VRs)**—areas where only a subset of the array receives significant energy from a specific user. By processing signals locally and using SIC, the receiver efficiently manages the uneven energy distribution across the array.

---

### **Key Takeaways**

*   **Near-Centralized Performance:** Numerical simulations show that this decentralized approach achieves a Symbol Error Rate (SER) very close to optimal centralized receivers (like Centralized ZF or VMP) while requiring significantly less centralized infrastructure.
*   **Superior Scalability:** Because LPUs only communicate with their immediate neighbors, the array can be scaled up (adding more antennas and LPUs) without a linear increase in the complexity of the interconnects or the burden on a central processor.
*   **Robustness to Non-Stationarity:** The method outperforms existing distributed benchmarks (such as the "daisy-chain" approach), particularly in XL-MIMO scenarios where users are only visible to certain parts of the array. The local SIC mechanism allows "strong" sub-arrays to assist "weak" sub-arrays via message passing.
*   **Reduced Processing Delay:** By allowing all LPUs to work in parallel rather than waiting for a central unit or a long sequential chain of computations, the proposed architecture minimizes processing latency.
*   **Impact of Sub-array Size:** The paper notes a trade-off: while decentralization is beneficial, having too many sub-arrays with too few antennas (high $B$, low $M_b$) can eventually degrade performance because the local spatial resolution becomes too low to effectively separate users.
