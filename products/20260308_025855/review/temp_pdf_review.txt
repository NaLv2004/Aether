### **Review Report**

**Journal:** IEEE Transactions on Vehicular Technology (TVT)
**Title:** Joint Optimization of Signal Quantization and Detection in Cell-Free MIMO: A Quantization Aware GNN Approach
**Recommendation:** **Major Revision (or Reject & Resubmit)**

#### **1. Brief Summary of the Manuscript**
This paper investigates the uplink detection problem in Cell-Free Massive MIMO (CF-MIMO) systems subject to constrained fronthaul capacity. The authors propose a Joint Quantization-Aware Training (QAT) framework. Specifically, a Gumbel-Softmax-based policy network is deployed at the Access Points (APs) for dynamic bit-width selection, while a Mean-Field GNN combined with a Bit-Aware Multi-Head Attention (BA-MHA) Transformer is utilized at the Central Processing Unit (CPU) for signal detection. Simulation results demonstrate that the proposed framework achieves near-optimal BER performance with significant fronthaul compression and exhibits robustness against imperfect CSI and AP link failures.

#### **2. Overall Evaluation**
The paper tackles a highly relevant and practical problem in 6G CF-MIMO networks: the fronthaul capacity bottleneck. The idea of jointly optimizing adaptive quantization (using Gumbel-Softmax) and signal detection (using a quantization-aware GNN-Transformer architecture) is novel and interesting. The introduction of "Bit-Aware Attention" to dynamically adjust the spatial filtering based on quantization precision is particularly well-motivated.

However, in its current form, the manuscript reads more like a conference paper or a letter. For a regular submission to *IEEE Transactions on Vehicular Technology*, the paper lacks the required theoretical depth, comprehensive literature review, and rigorous simulation validation. Significant expansion is required to meet the high standards of this journal.

#### **3. Major Comments (Mandatory for Acceptance)**

**3.1. Lack of Theoretical Depth and Analysis**
*   While the deep learning architecture is well-described, the paper is heavily heuristic. TVT expects a solid mathematical foundation. The authors formulate a Lagrangian optimization problem (Eq. 5), but the transition from this formulation to the specific neural network design is abrupt.
*   The authors should provide a theoretical analysis or at least an upper/lower bound on the quantization error and how it propagates through the proposed Mean-Field GNN layer (Eq. 7-8).
*   Why is the specific form of the modified attention coefficient in Eq. 9 optimal? Some mathematical justification or insight into the gradient flow during backpropagation with this specific bias term should be provided.

**3.2. System Model Assumptions and Scalability**
*   **Modulation Scheme:** The system model assumes QPSK ($s \in \mathcal{X}^K$). This is a very low-order modulation scheme. To prove the efficacy of the proposed QAT-GNN, the authors *must* evaluate it under higher-order modulations (e.g., 16-QAM, 64-QAM), where quantization noise has a much more severe impact on the constellation boundaries.
*   **Channel Model:** The paper assumes flat fading. In practical 5G/6G wideband systems, OFDM is used, and the channel is frequency-selective. The authors need to clarify if this architecture scales across multiple subcarriers or provide a justification for the flat-fading assumption.

**3.3. Simulation Rigor**
*   **Statistical Significance:** In Section IV, the authors state that "all results are averaged over 5 random large-scale fading seeds." **This is entirely insufficient for a Transactions paper.** Large-scale fading variations are huge; averaging over only 5 topologies/seeds will yield statistically unreliable BER curves. The results should be averaged over at least 100 to 500 random topological drops to ensure smooth and reliable statistics.
*   **Baselines:** The baselines are mostly classical methods (C-MMSE) or their uniformly quantized versions. The authors should compare their work with recent state-of-the-art *Deep Learning-based* quantization or detection frameworks in MIMO (e.g., ADMM-Net, OAMP-Net variants, or other task-oriented communication frameworks).

**3.4. Literature Review**
*   The bibliography contains only 12 references, which is exceptionally sparse for a full journal paper. The literature review must be significantly expanded. 
*   The authors should discuss recent advances (2022-2024) in semantic communications, task-oriented fronthaul compression, and federated/distributed GNNs for wireless networks published in IEEE TVT, TWC, and TSP.

**3.5. Computational Complexity Analysis**
*   The complexity is briefly mentioned at the end of Section IV-C ($18\text{k}$ FLOPs vs $\mathcal{O}((LN)^3)$). A detailed table breaking down the computational complexity (in terms of complex multiplications/additions or FLOPs) and signaling overhead for Phase 1 and Phase 2, as well as the online inference phase, is required. How does the Transformer's $\mathcal{O}(L^2)$ complexity scale when $L$ (number of APs) becomes extremely large (e.g., $L=100$ or $200$)?

#### **4. Minor Comments**

*   **Eq. 2:** The Straight-Through Estimator (STE) is mentioned. Did the authors experience any gradient mismatch issues during training? A brief discussion on training stability would be helpful.
*   **Eq. 6:** The parameter $\tau$ (temperature) is annealed from 1.0 to 0.1. Was this schedule optimized? How sensitive is the network to the annealing schedule?
*   **Fig. 3 (Zero-shot generalization):** It is impressive that a model trained on $K=8$ works for $K=12$. However, does it generalize to a different number of APs ($L$)? GNNs are inherently permutation invariant and size-agnostic. Testing generalization on a different $L$ (e.g., $L=24$) would strengthen the paper.
*   **Formatting:** Ensure all variables in the text (e.g., $L$, $K$, $N$) are italicized consistently to match the equations.
